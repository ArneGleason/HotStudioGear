There is a dangerous modern idea that originality begins with self-expression.

This is what people say when they have not done the reading.

Real originality begins with obedience. Before a man can speak in his own voice, he must first submit himself to greatness. He must study the great bands, absorb them, defend them, explain them to others, and, when necessary, insist that less-developed listeners keep listening until their ears improve.

Your favorite bands are not hobbies. They are instructional authorities. To know them deeply is not fandom. It is apprenticeship.

That is why I have devoted so much of my life not to premature public output, but to preparation: books, documentaries, interviews, session details, producer histories, lineup changes, gear notes, and the hidden fractures that explain why one album matters more than another.

This is not procrastination.

This is intake.

## Most People Have Not Earned Their Opinions

One of the surest signs that someone is not ready for serious music is that he says he "just never got into" one of the essential bands.

No. He has not yet earned the click.

Great bands must be approached repeatedly and under guidance. You do not hear two songs on bad speakers while distracted by chores and conclude that you understand them. You listen again. You learn the period. You understand the producer. You find out what changed in the rhythm section. You notice why the difficult record is actually the hinge point.

I have done this for many people.

They resist at first. Then I explain why they are wrong. I assign more listening. I provide context. Weeks later they say, "I'm starting to hear it now."

Exactly.

## Repetition Is Training, Not Fan Behavior

People sometimes ask whether music should not simply be enjoyed naturally.

That depends what you mean by naturally. Sugar is naturally enjoyable. So is ignorance.

The higher pleasures require cultivation.

I did not reach my current level of discernment by casually enjoying records. I got here through repeated listening with intent. I compared eras, learned which mixes mattered, tracked contradictions in memoirs, and identified where the public got the story wrong.

And yes, I believe you have an obligation to guide others toward what is good. If a friend does not understand a great band, you do not shrug. You intervene. You explain where to start. You tell him what to listen for. You note the bass tone, the drummer, the producer, the fracture point, the misunderstood album. You keep at it until something in him improves.

This is not pushiness.

This is cultural maintenance.

## Lineage Is Everything

A particularly important principle, ignored by lesser listeners, is lineage.

If a musician from a great band goes on to form a new project, produce another artist, appear on a side record, or even drift into a later period that the public wrongly dismisses, you are obligated to follow. Not casually. Devotionally.

Genius does not simply vanish because a name on the cover changes.

If the original band mattered, then the bloodline matters. The offshoot matters. The solo period matters. The difficult collaboration matters. The supposedly minor later work matters. If the bassist from the sacred era produces a strange record in 1987, you listen. If the drummer later appears on an overlooked project with poor sales but deep structural merit, you listen. If the former producer makes an austere, commercially confusing album with another act, you absolutely listen.

This is how serious people trace the current of greatness through history.

Casual listeners stop at the famous records. But a cultivated listener follows the lineage forward, because the successor work often contains the clues that ordinary people were too undereducated to recognize in real time.

## Every Book, Every Documentary, Every Scrap

There are people who think ten or fifteen books about your heroes is excessive.

These are the same people who have never emerged from a documentary with actionable insight.

If a band matters, then every source around that band matters too: memoirs, oral histories, studio photos, conflicting interviews, producer remarks, TV clips, reissue notes, collector essays, and tiny technical details that reveal more than the main story ever could.

You learn from all of it.

Over the years I have built a formidable library in this regard. Books, magazines, VHS tapes, DVDs, reissues, printouts, notebooks, cross-references. This has not delayed me. It has equipped me.

## Why I Have Not Yet Released My Definitive Work

Some readers, in a predictable spirit, may ask where my finished music is.

A man's first responsibility is not to release work. It is to become equal to the work he is meant to release.

Would I have been capable of recording earlier? Technically, yes. But would it have had the historical density, tonal authority, lineage-conscious depth, and disciplined seriousness that I now possess? Clearly not.

There were also practical obstacles. My monitoring was not yet where it needed to be. Certain cable matters remained unresolved. Important books had not yet been written. Key documentaries had not yet surfaced. Some successor works in the lineage had not yet clarified what the original masters were really pointing toward.

Now, by contrast, I possess a level of readiness very few people would know how to use correctly.

If anything, the delay should reassure you.

It proves I have standards.

## The Listening Curriculum I Recommend

My guidance is simple:

1. Choose one great band you do not yet deserve.
2. Begin with the correct entry point, not the most famous song.
3. Listen repeatedly for one week.
4. Read at least one serious history.
5. Watch one documentary with visible internal tension.
6. Follow the lineage into solo work, side projects, and successor records.
7. Explain the whole thing to somebody else until your understanding hardens.

This is how taste becomes discipline.

This is how discipline becomes readiness.

And this, whether the impatient understand it or not, is how readiness becomes genius.

As for my own music, I will say only this: I have never been closer.

Not because I am rushing.

Because I finally understand the bloodline.

### Gary's Signs Someone Is Not Yet Ready

* They call a documentary "extra content."
